<h2 id="brain-analogy">Brain analogy</h2><p>ANN's are one of the most popular ML approaches. A neural network can be defined as a model of reasoning based on the human brain.  The brain consists of basic information-processing units, called <strong>neurons</strong>. Between those neurons we have <strong>synapses. </strong>By using multiple neurons simultaneously, the brain perform its functions much faster than the fastest computers in existence.</p><p><br></p><p>Information is stored and processed in a neural network simultaneously throughout the whole network, rather than at specific locations. So, both data and its processing are global rather than local.</p><p><br></p><p>An ANN also works like this, neurons connected to each other and passing signals over weighted links from one to the other. <a href="https://cshub.nl/post/483276132#multi-layer-perceptrons" target="_blank">See the ML post about MLPs.</a> This post will contain quite similar content but with a bit different notation.</p><p><br></p><h2 id="neurons">Neurons</h2><p>Neurons consist of a few elements:</p><p><img src="https://i.imgur.com/VWx20ns.png" width="397"></p><p><em>Image taken from Artificial Intelligence, Negnevitsky</em></p><p><br></p><p>The neuron computes the weighted sum of the input signals and compares the result with a <strong>threshold value </strong><span class="ql-formula" data-value="\theta">﻿<span contenteditable="false"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>θ</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">\theta</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.69444em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span style="margin-right: 0.02778em;" class="mord mathdefault">θ</span></span></span></span></span>﻿</span>.
