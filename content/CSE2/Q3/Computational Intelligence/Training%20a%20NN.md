</div><p><br></p><h2 id="gradient-descent">Gradient descent</h2><p>We have a few types of GD:</p><p><br></p><h3 id="stochastic-gd">Stochastic GD</h3><p>You have 1 sample every iteration using which you use to estimate your gradient. This is fast to compute but gives a high variance (noise), which might mean slow convergence. This could also mean we move away from the global optimum.</p><p><br></p><p>On the other hand it could be useful, as for the same reason you could jump away from a local optimum, in the direction of the global optimum.</p><p><br></p><h3 id="batch-gd">Batch GD</h3><p>This is the opposite of stochastic GD, we use <span class="ql-formula" data-value="m">﻿<span contenteditable="false"><span class="katex"><span class="katex-mathml"><math><semantics><mrow><mi>m</mi></mrow><annotation encoding="application/x-tex">m</annotation></semantics></math></span><span class="katex-html" aria-hidden="true"><span class="base"><span class="strut" style="height: 0.43056em; vertical-align: 0em;"></span><span class="mord mathdefault">m</span></span></span></span></span>﻿</span> samples to compute our gradient. This is slower to compute, especially for large datasets. This will give us a low variance gradient estimation, which means faster convergence. This could mean you get stuck in a local optimum.</p><p><br></p><p>In practice we want to find a trade-off between SGD and BGD. This is called <strong>mini-batch GD</strong>. So we use a few samples to compute our gradient. A downside of this is that we have a new hyperparameter to control, the batch size.</p><p><br></p><h2 id="hyperparameter-tuning">Hyperparameter tuning</h2><p>A way to tune your hyperparameters is by training with a few different configurations and using the test set to determine which one was best. But this means that you are overfitting to the test set, which is also bad!</p><p><br></p><p>To tackle this, we use a validation set. <a href="https://cshub.nl/post/744858148#bootstrapping" target="_blank">See ML</a>.</p><p><br></p>
