This is classified into quality levels, the higher the better (stricter, less redundancy and better design quality)</p><p><br></p><p><strong>1NF</strong></p><ul><li>Restricts relations to being <em>flat, </em>meaning there is just a table which has proper attributes and tuples</li><li>The values of any attribute must be <em>atomic, </em>each attribute contains a single value</li></ul><p><br></p><p><strong>2NF</strong></p><ul><li>It must be in 1NF</li><li>No non-key attribute is functionally dependent on a proper subset of any candidate key. So all non-key attributes are fully dependent on the primary key</li></ul><p><br></p><p><strong>3NF</strong></p><ul><li>It must be in 2NF</li><li>Every entity type which is important has its own table</li><li>All non-key attributes are determined only by the candidate key and not by any other attributes</li></ul><p><br></p><p>The slides have great examples on these normal forms, <a href="https://gist.github.com/chiragchamoli/3754597" target="_blank">this </a>is also a great resource</p><p><br></p><p><strong>General observations</strong></p><p>We most often want schemas to be in 3NF or in BCNF, however there are cases when you would want higher or lower forms do make sure what you are doing when you choose something not 3NF or BCNF</p>
