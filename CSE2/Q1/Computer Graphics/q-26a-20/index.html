Given this roof:

  • Q1: How do you compute the roof's normal vectors per face? Take the cross product between two edges and normalize it
  • Q2: How do you compute the roofs normal vectors per vertex? Average the normals of the faces the vertex belongs to (just add the normals and renormalize)
  • Q3: What happens when you use Phong shading using this normal per vertex? The roof will appear smoothed, as a curved surface. This is because of the interpolated normals.
  • Q4: How do we solve this smoothing? At the points where faces meet and we have an averaged normal, we use two vertices with different normals. One of them is averaged, the other one is the normal of the face.


  • Q5: How do you design a camera that rotates around the house, looking at its center? Using the following numbers:
  • Your camera should stay at a distance ﻿ddd﻿ from the center of the house
  • The camera should be a distance ﻿hhh﻿ from the ground
  • Should rotate around the house at angle/frame


  • Step 1: find rotation to look at center (tilt), otherwise it will just be looking towards the ﻿zzz﻿ axis. This is done by taking the dot product of the direction it should be looking at and the current direction. Then you rotate the found ﻿θ\thetaθ﻿ around the ﻿xxx﻿-axis
  • Step 2: find translation (so find the eye position) such that the camera is at distance ﻿ddd﻿ from the house and height ﻿hhh﻿ from the ground. This can be done using Pythagoras.
  • Step 3: find rotation to go around the house. Rotate with ﻿ϕ\phiϕ﻿ (using which you can determine the speed) around the ﻿yyy﻿-axis

  • Q10: A mesh is represented by 3 arrays: 3D vertices, 2D texture coordinates and an in index array, in which 3 consecutive indices define a triangle.
  • Why is such a representation usually more efficient in memory than storing each triangle with 3 vertices and 3 2D texture coordinates? Sometimes triangles re-use vertices, so we only need to use an index to re-use a vertex.
  • For what models would it not matter? For models where very few vertices are shared.

  • Q13: What do we get when we take the cross product of two parallel vectors? We get the zero vector, as the cross product gives the normal of the plane that the two vectors span. If two vectors are parallel, then there is no normal (or infinite).


  • Q14: We want to achieve a comic look for our character, i.e. black when faces are opposing the light, white when they aren't. Define a function (pseudo code) that returns a color (RGB float triplet) and which takes normal, light position and vertex position as an input
  • First note that you can extend each triangle to a plane, which is orthogonal to the normal of the triangle. If the light source is on the direction of the normal, it will be white, if not, black
  • Calculating this can be done by calculating the vector from a vector from the triangle to the light and taking a dot product with the normal.
  • If this dot product ﻿&gt;0&gt;0>0﻿: white, ﻿&lt;0&lt;0<0﻿: black
