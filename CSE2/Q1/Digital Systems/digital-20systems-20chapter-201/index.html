In digital systems, it's very important to manage complexity. This complexity is often divided up into the layers visible above. Everything below (but often including) Digital circuits is generally the domain of electrical engineers.

Discipline is the act of intentionally restricting your design choices so that you can work more productively at a higher level of abstraction.

The 3 Ys

  • Hierarchy: dividing a system into modules that are easier to understand
  • Modularity: well defined functions of circuits which allow them to be connected together easily
  • Regularity: uniformity among modules. Common modules can be reused

Unity gain points

During the transition from high to low, there are two important points. They are the unity gain points, where the slope of the curve is -1. Above the first unity gain point, the value is treated as a 1, and below the second one it is treated as a 0. Everything in between is 'undefined behaviour'.

New Gate

A transition gate is like a transistor that can both provide a 0 value, a 1 value or a floating (disconnected) value.

CMOS Transistors

There are two kinds of CMOS transistors. One is called p-type, and the other n-type. Their names comes from the type of substrate they use. Either negatively doped, or positively doped.

nMOS transistors are normally off (no voltage is passed) but when you apply a voltage, they turn on. pMOS transistors are normally on (a voltage is passed) but when you apply a voltage, they turn off.

Pulling networks

Normally when creating logic gate circuits, you would build a pull up or pull down network. This is so you don't waste energy. The pull down network connects ground to the output, and the pull up network connects the supply voltage to the output. Ground and supply voltage should never be connected to the output at the same time as this would create a short circuit. Though sometimes this is done. The reasoning behind this is that generally pMOS transistors are slower than nMOS transistors, and one of the two networks predominantly contains one of the two. Furthermore, one of the networks will consist of more transistors in series, while the other will have more in parallel (parallel is obviously faster). Therefore it is sometimes useful to just omit one of the two networks and put a (small) resistor in it's place (to not create a huge short circuit). This is called Pseudo-nMOS logic.
