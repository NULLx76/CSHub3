Dimensionality reduction

We assume that we have a set of datapoints, that lie on a subspace of the dataset instead of being scattered around the entire space. The axis of this subspace are the effective representation of the data.


To find the real intrinsic dimension of a dataset, we use the rank of the matrix (the number of linearly independent columns of ﻿AAA﻿). If the rank is less than the amount of dimensions, we can reduce the dimension.


We might incur a bit of error because the points may not exactly lie on a line / plane etc.:

Image taken from Mining Massive Datasets


So why do we want to do dimensionality reduction?

  • We may discover hidden correlations/topics
  • We can remove redundant and noisy features
  • Interpretation and visualization
  • Easier storage and processing of data


Singular Value Decomposition (SVD)

Concept

We have:

  • ﻿AAA﻿: input data matrix, a ﻿m×nm\times nm×n﻿ matrix (e.g. ﻿mmm﻿ documents & ﻿nnn﻿ terms)
  • ﻿UUU﻿: left singular vectors, a ﻿m×rm\times rm×r﻿ matrix (e.g. ﻿mmm﻿ documents & ﻿rrr﻿ concepts, a small number)
  • ﻿Σ\SigmaΣ﻿: singular values, a ﻿r×rr\times rr×r﻿ diagonal matrix where ﻿rrr﻿ is the rank of ﻿AAA﻿
  • ﻿VVV﻿: right singular vectors, a ﻿n×rn\times rn×r﻿ matrix (e.g. ﻿nnn﻿ terms, ﻿rrr﻿ concepts)


This gives an SVD ﻿A≈UΣVTA\approx U\Sigma V^TA≈UΣVT﻿:

Image taken from Mining Massive Datasets


We can also express ﻿AAA﻿ as the sum of two vector multiplications: ﻿A≈∑iσiui∘viTA\approx\sum_i^{ }\sigma_iu_i\circ v_i^TA≈∑i​σi​ui​∘viT​﻿

Image taken from Mining Massive Datasets


According to the SVD theorem, it is always possible to decompose a real matrix ﻿AAA﻿ into ﻿UΣVTU\Sigma V^TUΣVT﻿where:

  • ﻿U, Σ, VU,\ \Sigma,\ VU, Σ, V﻿ are unique
  • ﻿U, VU,\ VU, V﻿ have orthonormal (unit length) columns
  • ﻿Σ\SigmaΣ﻿ is diagonal and is sorted in decreasing order

SVD vs Eigen-decomposition

  • SVD gives us ﻿A=UΣVTA=U\Sigma V^TA=UΣVT﻿
  • Eigen-decomposition gives us ﻿A=XΛXTA=X\Lambda X^TA=XΛXT﻿ where
  • ﻿AAA﻿ is symmetric
  • ﻿U,V,XU,V,XU,V,X﻿ are orthonormal
  • ﻿Λ, Σ\Lambda,\ \SigmaΛ, Σ﻿ are diagonal
  • We get: ﻿AAT=UΣVT(UΣVT)T=UΣVT(VΣTUT)=UΣΣTUT AA^T=U\Sigma V^T\left(U\Sigma V^T\right)^T=U\Sigma V^T\left(V\Sigma^TU^T\right)=U\Sigma\Sigma^TU^{T\ }AAT=UΣVT(UΣVT)T=UΣVT(VΣTUT)=UΣΣTUT ﻿, which can also be expressed as ﻿XΛXTX\Lambda X^TXΛXT﻿. So: ﻿λi=σi2\lambda_i=\sigma_i^2λi​=σi2​﻿!


Drawbacks

SVD is good in giving us the optimal low-rank approximation in terms of the Frobenius norm.


But, there is an interpretability problem as a singular vector specifies a linear combination of all inputs columns or rows. So many times, they are often hard to interpret, now we could easily interpret them as SciFi and Romance, but this is often not possible.


Another problem is lack of sparsity. Even if our matrix ﻿AAA﻿ is very sparse, our singular vectors are very dense which makes hard to compute:

CUR decomposition

The goal of a CUR decomposition is expressing ﻿AAA﻿ as a product of matrices ﻿C,U,RC,U,RC,U,R﻿ to again make the Frobernius norm ﻿∣A−C⋅U⋅R∣F\left|A-C\cdot U\cdot R\right|_F∣A−C⋅U⋅R∣F​﻿ small.


Here, we have some constraints:

  • The ﻿CCC﻿ matrix must contain columns of matrix ﻿AAA﻿
  • The ﻿RRR﻿ matrix must contain rows of matrix ﻿AAA﻿

Image taken from Mining Massive Datasets


How it works

We use the following algorithm to create a CUR decomposition:

Image taken from Mining Massive Datasets


Intuitively, it does the following:

  • For every column ﻿xxx﻿ we will compute its Frobenius norm and we will divide this by the total length (the sum of squared errors) of all the columns. So we ask: how long is this column w.r.t. the length of all other columns. This will give a probability distribution based on the sum of squared errors, the higher the SSE of the column, the higher the probability.
  • We iterate over amount of columns we want to select and we pick a column ﻿jjj﻿ based on ﻿P(x)P\left(x\right)P(x)﻿. Once we select a column we put it in matrix ﻿CCC﻿ after multiplying it with a value.


For rows, everything is the same.


We compute matrix ﻿UUU﻿ (the pseudoinverse)by:

  • letting ﻿WWW﻿ be the "intersection" of the samples columns ﻿CCC﻿ and rows ﻿RRR﻿, so rows and columns that are in both ﻿CCC﻿ and ﻿RRR﻿
  • computing the SVD of ﻿W=X⋅Z⋅YTW=X\cdot Z\cdot Y^TW=X⋅Z⋅YT﻿
  • computing ﻿U=W+=Y⋅Z+⋅XTU=W^+=Y\cdot Z^+\cdot X^TU=W+=Y⋅Z+⋅XT﻿ where ﻿Z+=1ZZ^+=\frac{1}{Z}Z+=Z1​﻿


Pros & Cons

Some pros are:

  • Easy interpretation: since the basis vectors are actual columns and rows
  • Sparse basis: since the basis vectors are actual columns and rows


A con is the fact that we have many duplicate columns and rows, but we can get rid of those by:

  • throwing them away
  • scale the columns/rows by the square root of the number of duplicates
