SDLC

SDLC is the process followed in SE for planning, designing, developing, testing, and deploying software projects


Waterfall model

  1. Requirements: capturing the functional and non-functional requirements in a product requirement document
  2. Design: software architecture, business rules
  3. Implementation: coding, development and integration
  4. Verification: testing and debugging
  5. Deployment


Pros

  • It is systematic and thorough, minimizing risks


Cons

  • Requirements: hard to elicit all the requirements from the beginning as users might not really know precisely what they need
  • Design: it is hard to decide which architecture to implement, under- and over-engineering can be a result
  • Implementation: in practice, this phase starts when the project is already late
  • Testing: testing is often too expensive
  • Deployment: changing something now is very costly, though this is the first time your customer really gets the product

Agile

Former (non-iterative) models have been strongly criticized over the decades due to their rigid structure and for focusing more on the software documentation rather than the actual implementation of software.


The four values of Agile are:

  • Individuals and interactions over processes and tools
  • Working software over comprehensive documentation
  • Customer collaboration over contract negotiation
  • Responding to change over following a plan


So Agile tries to make it as easy as possible for costumers to give feedback and thus also changes the release timeline, preferring shorter timescales.


This is done by daily collaboration between management and developers, which gives a good sustainable development and a constant pace. The teams also regularly reflect on how they can be more effective.


Scrum

Scrum is an iterative agile framework, which is based around sprints:

Image taken from slides by Mauricio Aniche


There are a few roles:

  • Product Owner (PO): maintains the backlog, communicates with the development team, bridges between the customer and the development team
  • Scrum Master: educates teams on the process, helps to keep the team on track and improve the process, removes impediments (hindrances)
  • Development team: develops the product, raise impediments, reflects and adapts


A sprint is an iteration, which takes about 2 to 4 weeks and produces a potentially shippable product. Deciding on what to do in a sprint is done during sprint planning:

  • The PO presents the most important item of the backlog (a prioritized list of to do's, can be bug reports or features).
  • The team discusses the item and estimates the required effort
  • Repeat until sprint is full


The team can estimate the required effort out of experience, empirical data or using planning poker. In planning poker, all team members simultaneously show a card that represents their estimate of that feature after the PO presented it. The authors with the lowest and highest estimation explain why, and we repeat this until a consensus is reached.


The features can be broken down into small tasks which should be highly technical (here the PO is not involved anymore), to aid the development afterwards. Using the Kanban board you can see which developer has picked up which task and what the status is of these tasks.


The team will have a short daily meeting in order to make sure everyone is on the same track.


In the end the team will do a sprint review with the PO:

  • The team presents the results of the sprint to the PO
  • The PO experiments and evaluates whether the done (the team should define what "done" means) features meet the acceptance criteria
  • The PO may propose to add new ideas to the backlog
  • The PO and the team decide to ship the current version or not


The team also does a sprint retrospective:

  • The team reflects about its behavior
  • The team decides on actionable points
  • The team reviews improvement from the last retrospective
