Ethics

Evaluation is any type of test/measure to see how well something performs. If your system interacts with people, your evaluation will nearly always also involve people! In that case, you need to take into account human-research ethics.


The Declaration of Helsinki - where a lot of human-research ethics are based on - defines the following:

  • Respect for the individual, right to make informed decisions (both before and during research). People should know what they're getting into and give explicit consent.
  • Welfare of participants takes precedence over interests of science and society. The importance of an experiment never outweighs the health of the individual
  • Vulnerable groups call for special vigilance
  • Research should be based on a theoretical background
  • Risks should never outweigh benefits
  • Ethical approval should be gained from independent committee


TU Delft HREC

The TU Delft Human Research Ethics Committee defines the following rules about minimal risk studies, i.e. the following studies are not minimal risk human research studies.


Vulnerable participants

If the study involves particularly vulnerable participants, or participants that are unable to give consent, e.g.:

  • Children
  • People with learning difficulties
  • Patients
  • People receiving counselling
  • People living in care or nursing homes
  • People recruited through self-help groups


Subordinate position

If the study involves participants in dependent or subordinate position to experimenter:

  • Students of experimenter
  • Children of experimenter


This is only allowed to happen if the teacher doesn't know who said yes/no.


Knowledge

If the study involves participants that take part without their knowledge:

  • Covert observation
  • Gathering of data without knowledge


By law you'd be allowed to film in public spaces but in minimal risk research studies you shouldn't do this.


Deceiving participants

If the study actively involves deceiving participants:

  • Deliberately falsely informing participants
  • Withholding information in such a way that they are likely to object or show unease when debriefed


This does not mean you always need to tell everything about the aims of your study.

  • Deceiving: Telling people you are studying facial expressions when you are really interested in what they say
  • Not deceiving: Telling people you are filming them without mentioning you are specifically interested in facial expressions


Sensitive personal data

If the study collects sensitive personal data:

  • Financial data
  • Location data
  • Data relating to children or other vulnerable groups
  • Religion
  • Race
  • Health
  • Sexual life
  • Political preference
  • Genetic and biometric data
  • Criminal offenses


You could ask this but there would need to be a really good reason for it.


Substances

If the study administers drugs, placebos or other substances. In HCI you are likely not asking this.


Blood / tissue samples

If in the study, blood / tissue samples are collected or there will be pain or more than discomfort, the study is not minimal risk.


Psychological risk

If the study risks psychological stress or anxiety beyond normally encountered


Main question: will you induce anxiety which people would not also risk in daily life? For example:

  • Showing people news clips: okay
  • Putting people in anxiety inducing situations they would otherwise never be in: not okay


Financial compensation

If the study involves financial inducement other than time/travel compensation:

  • Paying people for their time: okay
  • Giving them much more: not okay


Collecting identifiable data

If the study collects identifiable data (e.g. video, pictures, full names). If you still need this requires extra care in how you handle the data.


Uncertified devices

If the study uses uncertified devices (you may be testing out a new product that you designed).


Relevant for research on new (home-built) devices. However, computers, commercial robots & phones are not a problem


Informed consent

Informed consent’s main purpose:

  • Know & understand what you will have to do
  • Understand the conditions under which you agree (i.e. what rights you have)
  • Understand what data will be collected and how it is stored (i.e. how long, available for who)


It can look as follows:

  • Purpose of the research - to the extent that your research is not affected
  • Benefits and risks of participating - why is it important and are there any risks?
  • Procedures for withdrawal from the study - the experimenter doesn't need to know why
  • Whether any personal information about the participant will be collected, processed and how and for what purpose; the right of the participant to request access to and rectification or erasure of personal data
  • Usage of the data during research, safeguarding personal information, maintaining confidentiality and de-identifying (anonymising) data, controlled access to data, especially in relation to data archiving and reuse, ways of dissemination, data archiving and possible publishing
  • Retention period for the research data, or if that is not possible, criteria used to determine that period
  • Contact details of the researcher (or his/her representative), contact details of the data protection officer, institution, funding source, how to file a complaint.


Ethics procedure

  • Fill in TU Delft HREC Checklist
  • Describe what you will be doing with whom
  • Describe possible foreseen risks, and what you will do to reduce them
  • Make an informed consent-form


Evaluation

Evaluation is a part of the process, it's not about just evaluating the final product. Evaluation is part of a cycle: even after releases you have updates which need to be evaluated as well.


It involves asking yourself

  • Does this do what it was meant to do?
  • What works better?
  • What doesn't work?
  • What effect does it have?


Before you start evaluating, the following questions need to be answered:

Question Result Remarks
What do I want to know? Research question / hypothesis
Who will participate? Participants Think about restrictions, e.g. age
What will they do with what? Procedure / materials What will the participants do with which materials?
What do I measure? Measures What exactly will I measure?
How will my results answer my question? Analysis + results How does what I measure answer what I want to know?
What was the value? Discussion (insights) What is the value of knowing these results?
What will I then do? Discussion (actionable outputs)
What could I have done better? Discussion (lessons learned)


Expert evaluation

In expert evaluation, you talk with:

  • Usability experts
  • Experts in the field that you are working with (e.g. teachers when designing applications for kids)


The advantages is that it's relatively quick and cheap, but it's not a real life experience since you're not actually querying the user group.


Cognitive walkthrough

Cognitive Walkthrough is an evaluation method for manually simulating user’s cognitive processes as the user interacts with an user interface in an effort to accomplish a specific task.


Here you think about what a user is thinking, seeing, etc.


Some characteristics:

  • Early in the design cycle
  • Evaluate the ease with which users can perform a task with little or no formal instruction of informal coaching
  • Usually performed by expert in cognitive psychology


The steps are as follows:

  1. Define the steps that a user would need to take to achieve a goal
  2. Prepare a task description
  3. Action sequence
  4. Anticipated users
  5. Users's initial goals
  6. Ask questions about the design
  7. Will people try to achieve the right effect?
  8. Will they notice the correct actions?
  9. Will they associate the action with the effect?
  10. Will people see progress towards the goal after said action?


Here our goal is to identify the problem, but not yet fix it.


Some important factors about cognitive walkthroughs are:

  • Understand the user group
  • Have concrete scenarios of common/critical activities
  • Have a complete description of interface & correct action sequences


The validity of the results are better than using a plain heuristic, but worse than querying actual users.

It takes a lot of time however, since it requires a lot of in detail preparation. Therefore it may still be better to do heuristic evaluation.


Heuristic evaluation

Experts (about 5) independently evaluate the user interface whether it conforms to established usability principles (heuristics, see lecture 3):

  • The evaluation lasts about 1 or 2 hours
  • Method can be applied on paper prototypes
  • Output of the evaluation is a list of usability problems in the interface with reference to usability principles that were violated


After finding these problems, you use a severity matrix to help you prioritize problems: how much impact does a problem have on the usability / likeability of an application?

Ideally:

  • The experts are not the designers of the systems.
  • You'd evaluate it with 5 experts in parallel, fix stuff and do another round. However, when doing sequential evaluation you may require more rounds.
  • You'd use concrete examples & personas


Data analytics

Here you look at how things are used instead of asking end users. It's very objective and easy to gather. However, it may not be very useful and privacy infringing. Besides, it doesn't tell you why this behavior exists.


Connections

We can use correlations to find connections between data. There may be a connection to user age and time spent for example.


However, correlation does not always equal causation.


Heat map

Using click data, mouse-movement and scroll data we can create a heat map of a product:


This would answer the question whether people find the items that we find important, and find out where a process goes wrong.


Eye tracking

If you have eye-tracking data you can make a better heat map, however this is not easy to obtain.


It shows where all attention goes and how long this attention is retained.


Quantified self

These are personal analytics: you define yourself by collecting statistics. Examples of this are smart watches.


Sentiment analysis

You may get sentiment from text, using text processing. You obtain the opinions, beliefs, emotions and sentiments from the text.
