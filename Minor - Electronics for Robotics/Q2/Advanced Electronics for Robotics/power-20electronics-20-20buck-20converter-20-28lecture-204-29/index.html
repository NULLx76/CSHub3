Time domain analysis for inductor/capacitor

Inductors and capacitors are important because they are needed to store energy.


Inductor

As long as there is a voltage over the inductor, the current linearly increases/decreases. The slope of the change in current is equal to ﻿(Vend−Vstart)/L\left(V_{end}-V_{start}\right)/L(Vend​−Vstart​)/L﻿.


In an ideal inductor, we assume that the received and delivered energy is equal: volt-second balance, so the average voltage across an inductor is ﻿000﻿.


Capacitor

As long as there is a current over the capacitor, the voltage linearly increases/decreases. The slope of the change in voltage is equal to ﻿(Iend−Istart)/C\left(I_{end}-I_{start}\right)/C(Iend​−Istart​)/C﻿.


In an ideal capacitor, we assume that the received and delivered energy is equal: ampere-second balance, so the average current across a capacitor is ﻿000﻿.

Simplified model - Boundary Conduction Mode (BCM)

If we keep increasing the resistance, then since the ﻿V0V_0V0​﻿ must stay constant, the current will keep decreasing. At some point, the current will hit ﻿000﻿ at the peak of the cycle, but not stay on ﻿000﻿. This is boundary conduction mode.

The red part is at the boundary conduction model. The equations of continuous conduction mode still work.


Boundary conduction mode has a special property: the peak current is exactly double the average current.

We can use this equation to find the minimum inductance that our inductor should have, if we want to stay in continuous conduction mode:

Simplified model - Discontinuous Conduction Mode (DCM)
