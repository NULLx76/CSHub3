Transmission-Line Theory

You need to study transmission line theory if the wavelength is much smaller than circuit size.

If the wavelength is much larger, you can use the quasi-static approximation (circuit analysis).


A transmission line is a two-port network that connects a generator to a load:

It is a structure that carries alternating current and electromagnetic waves from one place to another. 


Types of TX-lines

  • very efficient

  • low losses
  • can handle very high power without melting the dielectric


EM waves

Transmission lines can operate in different modes:

  • Transverse electric: electric field lines are perpendicular to the propagation direction
  • Transverse magnetic: magnetic field lines are perpendicular to the propagation direction
  • Transverse electromagnetic: both field lines are perpendicular to the propagation direction


We only consider the last type in this course.


Voltage & current vs ﻿EEE﻿ & ﻿HHH﻿

In a transmission line, we are dealing with electric & magnetic fields. When studying transmission lines, we consider only voltage and current.

The relation between the two is as follows: In bounded problems, the field is a summation of modes. This yields

  • E(r)=∑mVm(z)em(x,y,km)\bm E(\bm r)=\sum_m V_m(z)\bm e_m(x,y,k_m)E(r)=∑m​Vm​(z)em​(x,y,km​)

Modes are vector functions that represent the distribution of the field in the plane transverse to the direction of propagation. In most practical applications we only use 1 mode, so the field has a specific shape that is described by only that one term: fundamental mode.


In this case, the amplitudes of the dominant node determine the voltage and current.

Standing waves

If there is a wave going forward and a wave going backward of the same amplitude and frequency, you get a standing wave (it looks like it's standing still, not moving left or right) of double the amplitude:

They quantify how much reflection there is, in this case it's very bad. If they don't have the same amplitude, the sum will oscillate between a max and a min.


To measure how many standing waves are present, we use the voltage standing wave ratio (VSWR): ﻿VSWR=Vmax⁡Vmin⁡=1+∣Γ∣1−∣Γ∣VSWR=\frac{V_{\max}}{V_{\min}}=\frac{1+\left|\Gamma\right|}{1-\left|\Gamma\right|}VSWR=Vmin​Vmax​​=1−∣Γ∣1+∣Γ∣​﻿. Its minimum is ﻿111﻿, when ﻿Γ= 0\Gamma=\ 0Γ= 0﻿.
