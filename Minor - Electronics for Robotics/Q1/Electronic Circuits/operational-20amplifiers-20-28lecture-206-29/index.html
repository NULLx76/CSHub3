6.4/6.5 Trans-impedance amplifier

This is a current-to-voltage amplifier, aka trans-impedance amplifier. It means that the gain of the op-amp circuit is an impedance.


We know there is ﻿000﻿ input voltage in a feedback network, so across the upper resistor we have a current flowing.


The feedback network only works when the current flowing through the upper resistor compensates the input current.


Adding signals

  • Adding voltages is difficult:
  • Voltages must be put in series
  • All but one source must be floating (you cannot ground ﻿V2V_2V2​﻿ and ﻿V3V_3V3​﻿)
  • Floating voltage sources are difficult to realize

  • Adding currents is easy
  • Currents must be put in parallel
  • All sources can be grounded

Integrator

The integrator circuit outputs the integral of the input signal over a frequency range based on the circuit time constant and the bandwidth of the amplifier.

There are two parts in this circuit. The right part is the feedback part, the voltage is measured by the feedback network and turned into a current


It is basically a trans-impedance amplifier, but with a capacitor instead of a resistor


We know that for a capacitor: ﻿Vo=−1C∫0tIrdtV_o=-\frac{1}{C}\int_0^tI_rdtVo​=−C1​∫0t​Ir​dt﻿ with ﻿IR=ViRI_R=\frac{V_i}{R}IR​=RVi​​﻿ . Since at the middle junction, there is always ﻿0V0V0V﻿, the output will be a nice voltage equal to the integral of the current.
