8.1 Bipolar Transistor

A transistor is used to amplify or switch electronic signals and electrical power.


A bipolar transistor is current controlled or voltage controlled current source

It consists of:

  • ﻿ccc﻿: collector
  • ﻿bbb﻿: base (control input)
  • ﻿eee﻿: emitter


Idea

I will first illustrate the idea behind it based on this video (much clearer than the lecture + slides!) and then get more into the theory (which will then be much clearer):

  • Remember the diode, that had an ﻿NPNPNP﻿ junction and if you connected the battery in the right way, it would be forward biased and current started to flow
  • Now we have a transistor, which can be seen as two "reverse" diodes squashed together.
  • If you only connect one power source over the entire circuit, there will always be a side with reverse bias.This is why we connect two:

  • The second power source makes the left ﻿NPNPNP﻿ junction a forward biased diode, thus a high number of electrons will flow from left to right (to the ﻿PPP﻿ part)
  • Some of the electrons will flow back through the upper power source, but because the ﻿PNPNPN﻿ junction on the right is forward biased, many more will flow towards the lower battery:

  • Because the ﻿PPP﻿ region is narrow, very few electrons will flow to the positive terminal of the 2nd power source
  • Now we can see that a small base current (second power supply) is amplified to a high collector current. Increasing the base current will linearly increase the collector current.

  • Depending on the voltage of the applied voltage, the transistor will be on/off. If the input voltage is not sufficient to overcome the barrier potential, it will be off.


Basic operation

We have some silicon with a ﻿nnn﻿ (free electrons) , ﻿ppp﻿ (not enough electrons, holes) and ﻿nnn﻿ doped area respectively, so we have 2 depletion layers:

Base-emitter

For a transistor to properly function, the two ﻿pnpnpn﻿ junctions must be properly biased. The base-emitter junction behaves like any other PN junction when viewed alone. 

  • If the base-emitter junction is forward biased, the transistor is on (causes electrons to flow from ﻿eee﻿ to bias ﻿bbb﻿).
  • If it is reverse biased, the transistor is off. 


This is just like a diode. If you forward bias a diode, the diode conducts. If you reverse bias the diode, the diode cuts off.


Base-collector

The base-emitter junction in a transistor is going to essentially turn the transistor on or off, the base-collector junction doesn't influence that.


The base-collector is reverse-biased, causing electrons to flow from ﻿bbb﻿ to ﻿ccc﻿. The electrons coming from ﻿eee﻿ are "pulled through" the ﻿BCBCBC﻿ junction by the electric field:

8.2 PNP transistors

It is similar to a NPN transistor, but if you bias it in the opposite way, the current flow in a different direction. This means it is useful for negative currents.


8.3 Field-effect transistor (FET)

You'll find these transistors (voltage controlled current source) in analog, but mostly digital applications: they are extremely small, so you can pack immense amounts of them together.


There are two forms:

  • Insulated gate FET (MOSFET), the most used transistor
  • Junction gate FET, it can handle high radiation


A FET has 3 terminals:

  • gate ﻿ggg﻿, the control input
  • source ﻿sss﻿, where the signal starts
  • drain ﻿ddd﻿, where the signal leaves


8.4 MOSFET

We have two types of MOSFETs, the ﻿nnn﻿ and ﻿ppp﻿ types. Here we drew an ﻿nnn﻿ type MOSFET.


You have a piece of silicon of ﻿nnn﻿ type and we doped a ﻿ppp﻿ region. The current flows through the ﻿nnn﻿ channel. Next to the channel we have an oxide layer, insulating it from the metal gate electrode.


Gate

If we put a positive voltage on the gate:

  • The positive voltage attracts electrons
  • The channel becomes thicker
  • The resistance of the channel is reduced
  • The channel is enhanced


If we put a negative on the gate the opposite happens:

  • The negative voltage repels electrons
  • The channel becomes thinner
  • The resistance of the channel is increased
  • The channel is depleted


So the voltage from the gate controls the resistance: so it is a voltage-controlled resistance. The connection to the ﻿ppp﻿ layer (back-gate) makes sure the ﻿pnpnpn﻿ junction is not conducting (since there is ﻿0V0V0V﻿ across the junction).

8.5 JFET

Insulated gate with a reverse-biased ﻿pnpnpn﻿ junction.


Mostly used in analog applications:

  • low-noise
  • radiation-hard circuits
  • cryogenic temperature operation


Operation

If we make the reverse-bias higher, the depletion layer becomes wider and the channel shrinks and thus its resistance increases.

So we need to take into account that if we accidentally forward-bias this junction (the gate is more positive than the channel), we'll get a gate current and that'll destroy the JFET.


We thus need to make sure it is always reversed-biased, the gate-channel voltage must always be negative.

8.6 FET with drain voltage

In this picture, we have a gate voltage, bus also a drain voltage. We can now take a look at the difference between the source and channel voltage.


If you bias correctly, the voltage difference between the drain and gate, is larger than the gate and ground. The higher the drain voltage, the smaller the channel.

8.7 Channel-length modulation

There is still some output resistance in our transistor:

On the left you see a pinch-off with a relatively low drain voltage, so the part where there's no channel is relatively short.

  • This means that it is more difficult for the electrons to get to the other side


On the right you see a pinch-off with a relatively high drain voltage, so the part where there's no channel is quite long.

  • This means that the electric field is larger, so it is more likely that an electron ends up at the gate and thus we'll have a higher current


So we can model the length of the channel as an output resistance.


8.8 Power MOSFETs

Even though it would appear to be Coefficient desirable to use a very large MOSFET for an application such as a hot swap and limit it to a low current, it may be an inappropriate approach.

Use of a very-low-on-resistance device offers low losses for steady-state operation but may cause the device to fail during a short circuit or an overload.


I.e.: don't swap a high-resistance MOSFET for a low-resistance one, as it will become thermally unstable.

8.11 Active devices

In general, transistors:

  • Are voltage-controlled current sources (FET, Bipolar)
  • Are current-controlled current sources (bipolar, accurate, but in principle undesired)
  • N and P devices have the same small signal models (signs are equal)


We have three types:

  • Bipolar transistor
  • Very good current source, exponential transfer from input to output (more gain)
  • Conduction through junctions
  • Thermally unstable


  • MOSFET (used the most)
  • Quadratic transfer from input to output
  • Thermally stable depending on bias conditions
  • Perfect for digital circuits


  • JFET
  • Quadratic transfer from input to output
  • Thermally stable
