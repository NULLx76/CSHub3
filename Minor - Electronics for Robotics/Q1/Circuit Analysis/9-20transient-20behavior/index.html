9.1 Introduction

The behavior of a circuit given fixed DC signals or constant AC signals are referred to as the steady-steady response of a system.


We will now look at how circuits react before they reach this response: if they are turned on / off.

9.5 Second-order systems

Circuits that contain both capacitance and inductance are normally described by second-order differential equations. The equation for an RLC circuit would be ﻿Ldidt+Ri+vc=VL\frac{di}{dt}+Ri+v_c=VLdtdi​+Ri+vc​=V﻿.


Since ﻿iii﻿ is equal to the current in the capacitor (﻿CdvdtC\frac{dv}{dt}Cdtdv​﻿), we can differentiate this with respect to ﻿ttt﻿ to obtain ﻿LCd2vcdt2+RCdvcdt+vc=VLC\frac{d^2v_c}{dt^2}+RC\frac{dv_c}{dt}+v_c=VLCdt2d2vc​​+RCdtdvc​​+vc​=V﻿, which is a second-order differential equation.

  • ﻿ωn\omega_nωn​﻿ being the undamped natural frequency
  • ﻿ζ\zetaζ﻿ being the damping factor


You can see here how such systems respond to a step function. Small values of ﻿ζ\zetaζ﻿ cause the system to respond more rapidly, but ﻿ζ&lt;1\zeta&lt;1ζ<1﻿ cause the system to overshoot. When ﻿ζ=1\zeta=1ζ=1﻿, the system is critically damped (ideal case). For ﻿ζ&gt;1\zeta&gt;1ζ>1﻿, the system will be overdamped.


When ﻿ζ=0\zeta=0ζ=0﻿, it will be undamped, giving a continuous oscillation of the output with natural frequency ﻿ωn\omega_nωn​﻿.
