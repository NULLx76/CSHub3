6.3 Reactance of inductors and capacitors

The ratio of the voltage to the current, ignoring any phase shift, is a measure of how the component opposes the flow of electricity.


The part of this ratio that is constant, regardless of frequency, is resistance ﻿RRR﻿

The part of this ratio which varies with frequency due to capacitance and inductance, is reactance ﻿XXX﻿


Generally, they are called impedance (see 6.5)


﻿XXX﻿So:

  • For resistors: ﻿XR=0X_R=0XR​=0﻿ (but ﻿RR=RR_R=RRR​=R﻿), as resistors have the same impedance regardless of frequency
  • For inductors: ﻿XL=ωLX_L=\omega LXL​=ωL﻿ (and ﻿RL=0R_L=0RL​=0﻿), as inductors have no constant impedance
  • For capacitors: ﻿XC=1ωCX_C=\frac{1}{\omega C}XC​=ωC1​﻿ (and ﻿RC=0R_C=0RC​=0﻿)

6.5 Impedance

As said in 6.3, impedance is the relationship between the current and voltage. The impedance does not only represent the magnitude, but also the phase. This means that impedance changes with frequency.


The magnitude of the impedance is given by ﻿Z=R2+X2Z=\sqrt{R^2+X^2}Z=R2+X2​﻿. The phase angle is given by ﻿ϕ=tan⁡−1XR\phi=\tan^{-1}\frac{X}{R}ϕ=tan−1RX​﻿.

You can represent the impedance in a rectangular form, as a resistive plus reactive component, or in complex polar form by giving the magnitude ﻿ZZZ﻿ and angle ﻿ϕ\phiϕ﻿.
