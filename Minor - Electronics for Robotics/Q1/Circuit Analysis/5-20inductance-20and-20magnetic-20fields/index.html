5. Inductance and Magnetic Fields

5.1 Introduction

Capacitors store energy by producing an electric field within a piece of dielectric material. Inductors also store energy, but in this case it is stored within a magnetic field.


5.2 Electromagnetism

A wire carrying an electrical current causes a magnetomotive force (m.m.f.), ﻿FFF﻿, which produces a magnetic field around it. This is similar to the e.m.f. of an electrical circuit, which results in an electric fields and produces electric current.


Magnetic field

The magnetic field strength ﻿HHH﻿ is given by ﻿H=FlH=\frac{F}{l}H=lF​﻿ ﻿[A/m]\left[A/m\right][A/m]﻿, with:

  • ﻿FFF﻿ is the m.m.f, for simple wires ﻿F=IF=IF=I﻿
  • ﻿lll﻿ is the length


The direction of the magnetic field is determined by the direction of the current in the wire. The field is directional and is clockwise with the flow:


Magnetic flux

The magnetic field produces a magnetic flux that flows in the same direction as the field. Magnetic flux is given the symbol ﻿ϕ\phiϕ﻿, and the unit of flux is the weber (﻿WbWbWb﻿).


The strength of the flux at a particular location is measured in terms of the magnetic flux density, ﻿B=ϕAB=\frac{\phi}{A}B=Aϕ​﻿﻿BBB﻿﻿[T=Wbm2]\left[T=\frac{Wb}{m^2}\right][T=m2Wb​]﻿, which is the flux per unit area of cross-section.


Using ﻿BBB﻿ we can thus also compute the flux itself: ﻿ϕ=BA\phi=BAϕ=BA﻿.


Strength

The flux density ﻿BBB﻿ at a point depends on the strength of the field at that point, but it is also greatly affected by the material present. In air, there is less flux than in an ferromagnetic ring.


This yields the expression ﻿B=μHB=\mu HB=μH﻿, with:

  • ﻿HHH﻿ ﻿[A/m]\left[A/m\right][A/m]﻿ being the magnetic field strength


Coil

When a current-carrying wire is formed into a coil,  the magnetic field is concentrated within the coil, and it increases as more and more turns are added: ﻿F=INF=INF=IN﻿, with:

  • ﻿III﻿ ﻿[A]\left[A\right][A]﻿ being the current
  • ﻿NNN﻿ being the amount of turns


The unit of ﻿NNN﻿ is sometimes informally given as “turns”, and then ﻿FFF﻿ has the unit “ampere-turns”.


In a long coil with many turns, most of the magnetic flux passes through the centre of the coil. It then follows that the magnetic field strength produced by such a coil is given by ﻿H=INlH=\frac{IN}{l}H=lIN​﻿.


5.3 Reluctance

An analogous concept to resistance with the emf exists, reluctance. In this case, the reluctance is a measure of how the circuit opposes the flow of magnetic flux.


This is also defined similarly to resistance: ﻿S=FΦS=\frac{F}{\Phi}S=ΦF​﻿, with:

  • ﻿SSS﻿ ﻿[A/Wb]\left[A/Wb\right][A/Wb]﻿ being the reluctance
  • ﻿FFF﻿ ﻿[A]\left[A\right][A]﻿ being the m.m.f.
  • ﻿Φ[Wb]\Phi\left[Wb\right]Φ[Wb]﻿ being the flux


5.4 Inductance

A changing magnetic flux induces an electrical voltage (an e.m.f.) in any conductor within the field.


Faraday’s law states: the magnitude of the e.m.f. induced in a circuit is proportional to the rate of change of the magnetic flux linking the circuit.

Lenz’s law states: the direction of the e.m.f. is such that it tends to produce a current that opposes the change of flux responsible for inducing that e.m.f.


Therefore, if a coil of ﻿NNN﻿ turns experiences a change in magnetic flux, then the induced voltage ﻿VVV﻿ is given by ﻿V=N dΦdtV=N\ \frac{d\Phi}{dt}V=N dtdΦ​﻿, where:

  • ﻿VVV﻿ is the voltage
  • ﻿NNN﻿ is the turns of the coil
  • ﻿dΦdt\frac{d\Phi}{dt}dtdΦ​﻿﻿[Wb/s]\left[Wb/s\right][Wb/s]﻿ is the rate of change of the flux


This property, whereby an e.m.f. is induced into a wire as a result of a change in magnetic flux, is referred to as inductance.


5.5 Self-inductance

So far we have seen that:

  • Current flowing in a coil / single wire produces a magnetic flux around it
  • Changes in the current will cause changes in the magnetic flux
  • Changes in magnetic flux induces an e.m.f.

From this it follows that when the current in a coil changes, an e.m.f. is induced in that coil which tends to oppose the change in the current, called self-inductance.


This yields ﻿V=L dIdtV=L\ \frac{dI}{dt}V=L dtdI​﻿, where:

  • ﻿VVV﻿ is the voltage across the inductor as a result of changes in the current
  • ﻿LLL﻿ ﻿[H]\left[H\right][H]﻿ is the inductance of the coil
  • ﻿dIdt\frac{dI}{dt}dtdI​﻿ ﻿[A/s]\left[A/s\right][A/s]﻿ is the change in current


The book uses a positive polarity for these self-inducted voltages.


5.6 Inductors

Inductors are circuit elements which are designed to provide inductance. They exist in all types of shapes and forms, with ferromagnetic cores etc.


The inductance of a coil is determined by its dimensions and by the material around which it is formed. It is computed as: ﻿L=μ0μrAN2lL=\frac{\mu_0\mu_rAN^2}{l}L=lμ0​μr​AN2​﻿. Note that ﻿μr\mu_rμr​﻿﻿=1=1=1﻿ for an inductor which inducts into a non-magnetic material.


Ideal inductor

Since a real inductor has resistance, we can model a real component as an ideal inductor as an inductor without resistance and a resistor in series.


Regular wires usually also have a small stray inductance that is often unwanted, and can really affect the circuit operation.

5.8 Relationship between voltage and current in an inductor

In 5.5, we saw that ﻿V=L dIdtV=L\ \frac{dI}{dt}V=L dtdI​﻿, implying that when a constant current is passed through an inductor (i.e. ﻿dIdt=0\frac{dI}{dt}=0dtdI​=0﻿), the voltage across it is zero.


The current through an inductor cannot change instantaneously, since this would correspond so an infinite change in current. Thus inductors tend to stabilise the current flowing through them (like capacitors stabilize voltage):

What we see is very similar to the capacitor:

  1. When the switch is open, the current across the inductor is initially ﻿000﻿
  2. When the switch is closed, the current starts off at ﻿000﻿, and since ﻿V=VR+VLV=V_R+V_LV=VR​+VL​﻿, ﻿VL=VV_L=VVL​=V﻿
  3. The voltage across the inductor makes sure there is a steady rise of the current. As ﻿III﻿ grows, ﻿VLV_LVL​﻿ shrinks and the rate ﻿dIdt\frac{dI}{dt}dtdI​﻿ also shrinks
  4. Eventually, the voltage over the inductor is virtually 0


As you can see, a higher resistance, will decrease the rate at which the circuit-approaches its steady rate. This rate will increase with a higher inductance (as ﻿V=L dIdtV=L\ \frac{dI}{dt}V=L dtdI​﻿), which is the opposite in comparison with capacitors.


Note that when we need to switch inductive loads, we should make sure to add extra circuitry to reduce the rate of change of the current.


5.9 Sinusoidal voltages and currents

If a sinusoidal current waveform is passed over an inductor, it creates a voltage waveform which lags behind by 90 degrees.

5.11 Mutual inductance

If two conductors are linked magnetically, then a changing current in one of these will produce a changing magnetic flux associated with the other and will result in an induced voltage in this second conductor. This is mutual inductance.


Mutual inductance is quantified in a similar way to self-inductance, such that, if a current ﻿111_111​﻿ flows in one circuit, the voltage induced in a second circuit ﻿V2V_2V2​﻿ is given by ﻿V2=M dI1dtV_2=M\ \frac{dI_1}{dt}V2​=M dtdI1​​﻿, where ﻿MMM﻿﻿[H]\left[H\right][H]﻿ is the mutual induction between the two circuits.


In transformers this is used, where a changing current in one coil is used to induce a changing current in a second coil. The degree of this coupling is the coupling coefficient, which defines the fraction of the flux of one coil that links with the other.


5.12 Transformers

In a transformer, we assume a coupling coefficient of ﻿111﻿, an ideal transformer. This means that all flux from the primary coil links with the secondary.


Since the magnetic flux associated with the primary coil is the same as that associated with the secondary coil, the output ratio between the two is solely dependent on their respective amount of coils: ﻿V2V1=N2N1\frac{V_2}{V_1}=\frac{N_2}{N_1}V1​V2​​=N1​N2​​﻿, called the turns ratio of a transformer.


Load

If we add a resistive load to the second circuit, a current will flow there, which will itself produce a magnetic flux. This flux will oppose the flux generated by the primary circuit, in turn reducing the voltage in htat coil.


The overall effect of this mechanism is that when the secondary is open circuit, or when the output current is very small, the output voltage is as predicted by the previous equation, but as the output current increases the output voltage falls.


Step up-down

If the secondary of a transformer has many more turns than the primary we have a step-up transformer, which provides an output voltage that is much higher than the input voltage, but it can deliver a smaller output current, and vice-versa.
