4. Capacitance and Electric Fields

4.2 Capacitors and capacitance

Capacitors consist of two conducting surfaces separated by an insulating layer called a dielectric. 


The e.m.f. produced by the battery attempts to drive a current around the network. Electrons flowing from the negative terminal of the battery will flow onto the lower plate of the capacitor, where they will repel electrons from the upper plate. As electrons are repelled from this plate, they leave a residual positive charge. The combined effect of these two processes is that electrons flow into the lower plate of the capacitor and flow out from the upper plate. This effect can not last indefinitely, since there are only a finite amount of electrons in the positive plate. Do note that the electrons are not flowing through the capacitor.


As electrons flow around the circuit they produce an increasing positive charge on one side of the capacitor and an increasing negative charge on the other. The result is an increasing electric field between the two plates. This produces a potential difference ﻿VcV_cVc​﻿ across the capacitor, which opposes the e.m.f. of the battery. We can use this potential difference like a little rechargeable battery.


For a given capacitor, the charge stored ﻿QQQ﻿ is ﻿Q=CVQ=CVQ=CV﻿, with:

  • ﻿QQQ﻿ the charge in coulombs
  • ﻿VVV﻿ the voltage in volts
  • ﻿CCC﻿ the capacitance in farads


4.3 Capacitors and alternating voltage and currents

Since the voltage across a capacitor is pro­portional to the charge on it, it follows that a changing voltage must correspond to a changing charge. The voltage produced by the alternating voltage source is constantly matched by both plates, thus AC current can flow through a capacitor.

As such, a capacitor can be compared with glass, with a high pressure on one side and low pressure on the other. This will cause the glass to bend a bit. When the pressure on both sides becomes equal (so the switch in the circuit is closed), this potential energy will be released and sound waves (so current) will be generated, while no air was passing through!

4.5 Electric field strength and electric flux density

EFS

Electric charges of the same polarity repel each other, while those of opposite polarities attract. When charged particles experience a force as a result of their charge, we say that an electric field exists in that region. The magnitude of the force is determined by the electric field strength E in a capacitor. For some reason (convention), a bold E is used for electric field strength. I will be calling it ﻿EFSEFSEFS﻿ for the rest of this summary. 


The ﻿EFSEFSEFS﻿ is defined as the force exerted on a unit charge at that point: ﻿EFS=VdEFS=\frac{V}{d}EFS=dV​﻿, with ﻿VVV﻿ being the voltage between two points a distance ﻿ddd﻿ apart.


Dielectric strength

All insulating materials have a maximal value for the ﻿EFSEFSEFS﻿ before they break down. This is called their dielectric strength ﻿EmE_mEm​﻿. For this reason, all capacitors have a maximum operating voltage. To obtain this maximum capacitance, we need to make our dielectric as thin as possible. However, this also increases the ﻿EFSEFSEFS﻿, so we need to make a compromise between physical size and breakdown voltage.


So: if our ﻿EFS&gt;EmEFS&gt;E_mEFS>Em​﻿, the electrons will actually pass through the dielectric!


Electric flux

The force between positive and negative charges is often described in terms of an electric flux linking them. This is measured using the same units as electric charge (coulombs) and thus a charge of ﻿QQQ﻿ coulombs will produce a total electric flux of ﻿QQQ﻿ coulombs. 


Flux density

We also define what is termed the electric flux density, D (again, bold. Will use ﻿EFDEFDEFD﻿ from now on), as the amount of flux passing through a defined area perpendicular to the flux. In a capacitor, the size of the plates is always much greater than their separation, so ‘edge effects’ can be ignored and we can assume that all the flux produced by the stored charge passes through the area of the dielectric.


The ﻿EFDEFDEFD﻿ is given by ﻿EFD=QAEFD=\frac{Q}{A}EFD=AQ​﻿, since the flux ﻿QQQ﻿ passes through an area ﻿AAA﻿.


If we combine the equations we have so far, we get that ﻿ϵ=EFDEFS\epsilon=\frac{EFD}{EFS}ϵ=EFSEFD​﻿. This means that how much capacitance a dielectric has, is its flux density (amount of flux per area) over the the field strength (force exerted on a unit charge).


Comparison

A comparison between the 3 terms mentioned here:

  • The electric flux can be seen as "lines" of electric fields emitted from a source
  • The electric field strength is the strength of the electric field at a certain point
  • The electric field density of the amount of "lines" passing through a certain area

4.7 Relationship between voltage and current in a capacitor

Ohm’s law does not hold for capacitors, as the voltage across a capacitor is directly related to the charge on the capacitor (﻿V=QCV=\frac{Q}{C}V=CQ​﻿). The charge is given by the integral of the current over time, so ﻿V=QC=1C∫IdtV=\frac{Q}{C}=\frac{1}{C}\int_{ }^{ }IdtV=CQ​=C1​∫​Idt﻿.


One implication of this relationship is that the voltage across a capacitor cannot change instantaneously, since this would require an infinite current. The rate at which the voltage changes is controlled by the magnitude of the current. For this reason, capacitors tend to stabilize the voltage across them.


We can also look at this relationship the other way around, as current being a function of voltage: ﻿I=CdVdtI=C\frac{dV}{dt}I=CdtdV​﻿.
