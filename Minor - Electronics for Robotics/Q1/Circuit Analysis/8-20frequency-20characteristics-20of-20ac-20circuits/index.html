8.4 Frequency response

The way in which the gain of a circuit changes with frequency is termed its frequency response.

These changes take the form of variations in the magnitude (amplitude response) of the gain and in its phase angle (phase response).

The corresponding angular and cyclic frequencies are denoted as ﻿ω0\omega_0ω0​﻿﻿=1LG=\frac{1}{\sqrt{LG}}=LG​1​﻿ and ﻿f0f_0f0​﻿﻿=12πLC=\frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{LC}}=2πLC​1​﻿.


Quality factor

At the resonant frequency, the impedance is at its minimum, so the current at its maximum.


The ratio of the energy stored to the energy dissipated (by the capacitors/inductors) during each cycle (at the resonant frequency) is called the quality factor ﻿QQQ﻿ of the circuit. Since the energy stored in the capacitor and inductor are equal, we can choose either of them to compute ﻿QQQ﻿:

  • Inductor: ﻿Q=I2XLI2R=XLRQ=\frac{I^2X_L}{I^2R}=\frac{X_L}{R}Q=I2RI2XL​​=RXL​​﻿
  • Capacitor: ﻿Q=I2XCI2R=XCRQ=\frac{I^2X_C}{I^2R}=\frac{X_C}{R}Q=I2RI2XC​​=RXC​​﻿


We can also write it as ﻿Q=VLVRQ=\frac{V_L}{V_R}Q=VR​VL​​﻿, or as the ratio between the voltage across ﻿LLL﻿ or ﻿CCC﻿ at the resonance frequency over the supply voltage.

8.13 Filters

RC & RL

We often use plain RC networks and not RL networks because inductors are bulky, and have a high loss. These simple filters are called first-order filters.


However, the ﻿666﻿ dB/octave attenuation is not enough for all applications, we may want a higher roll-off rate (e.g. 18 dB/octave). We could in theory achieve this by combining multiple stages, to create an ﻿nnn﻿-pole filter, having a cut-off slope of ﻿6n6n6n﻿ dB/octave, and producing a ﻿n×90∘n\times90^{\circ}n×90∘﻿ phase shift.


In the ideal case, we would have a constant gain and no phase shift inside our wanted range of frequencies (pass band) and zero gain outside. The transition between these two regions happens at the corner frequency ﻿f0f_0f0​﻿:

Although adding stages helps, it doesn't increase the sharpness of the knee.


RLC

We can also use an RCL network (second-order filter) that has a much sharper cut-off. They are also called tuned circuits.


Active filters

However, as noted earlier, inductors are not handy. We can construct a range of effective filters using op-amps, resistors & capacitor. Because of the use of an op-amp, they are called active filters.


In RC filters, our gain starts drop as we get nearer to ﻿f0f_0f0​﻿, so our gain is not constant. In active filters, our gain is also not constant, but because around ﻿f0f_0f0​﻿, the gain may actually be increased a bit:

Although all filters drop by a factor of ﻿6n6n6n﻿ dB/octave, the sharpness of the knee varies from one design to the other: a sharp knee tends to produce more variation in the gain of the filter within the pass band.


The phase response of the filter is also of great importance: whether the variation lags or leads with frequency as a signal passes through the filter.


We will go through a few filters now:

  • Butterworth filter: flat response within its pass band, with a less sharp knee and bad phase performance
  • Chebyshev filter: sharp knee, lots of variations within the pass band and bad phase performance (serious distortion)
  • Bessel filter: optimized for linear phase response (good!), better knee than RC filter. Since the phase response is linear, it seems like the resulting phase shift has a fixed time delay, with all frequencies delayed by the same time interval: no distortion.
