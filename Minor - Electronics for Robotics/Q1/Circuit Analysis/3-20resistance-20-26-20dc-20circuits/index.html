3.2 Current and charge

An electric current represents a flow of electric charge. The more charge is flowing, the higher the current: ﻿I=dQdtI=\frac{dQ}{dt}I=dtdQ​﻿, with ﻿QQQ﻿ being the charge in coulombs.


We can rearrange this as ﻿Q=∫IdtQ=\int_{ }^{ }IdtQ=∫​Idt﻿. A constant current results in a simple relationship ﻿Q=I×tQ=I\times tQ=I×t﻿.


At an atomic level, this represents a flow of electrons, each of which carries a negative charge of ﻿1.6⋅10−191.6\cdot10^{-19}1.6⋅10−19﻿ coulombs. This is why current in one direction actually represents the passage of electrons in the opposite direction.


3.3 Voltage sources

A voltage source produces an electromotive force (e.m.f., in volt), which causes a current to flow within a circuit. It is defined as the potential difference between two points when 1 joule of energy is used to move 1 coulomb of charge.


Real voltage sources such as batteries have an internal resistance, which limits the current that they can supply. In models we often use ideal voltage sources that have no resistance.

3.5 Resistance and Ohm's law

The voltage across a conductor is directly proportional to the current flowing in it, by Ohm's law: ﻿V=IRV=IRV=IR﻿, where:

  • ﻿VVV﻿ is voltage
  • ﻿III﻿ is current
  • ﻿RRR﻿ is resistance


The unit of resistance is Ohm (﻿Ω\OmegaΩ﻿), and can be defined as the resistance of a circuit in which a current of ﻿1A1A1A﻿ produces a potential difference of ﻿1V1V1V﻿.


Components designed to provide resistance in electrical circuits are called resistors. The resistance they provide is directly related to their dimensions:

  • Making a resistor twice as long is equivalent to connecting them in series, so ﻿R∝lR\propto lR∝l﻿
  • Doubling the cross-sectional area of a resistor is equivalent to connecting it in parallel, so ﻿R∝1AR\propto\frac{1}{A}R∝A1​﻿
  • The constant of the proportionality is the resistivity of the material, and given by ﻿ρ\rhoρ﻿

These three together yield the relationship ﻿R=ρlAR=\frac{\rho l}{A}R=Aρl​﻿.


Sometimes this resistivity is inversed, which yields the conductivity. Unfortunately, the resistance changes with temperature, depending on their temperature coefficient of resistance ﻿α\alphaα﻿.

3.8 Thevenin's theorem and Norton's theorem

Thevenin’s theorem: Any two-terminal network of resistors and energy sources can be replaced by a series combination of an ideal voltage source ﻿VVV﻿ and a resistor ﻿RRR﻿ where ﻿VVV﻿ is the open-circuit voltage and ﻿RRR﻿ is the resistance measured between the output terminals.


Norton’s theorem: Any two-terminal network of resistors and energy sources can be replaced by a parallel combination of an ideal current source ﻿III﻿ and a resistor ﻿RRR﻿, where ﻿III﻿ is the short-circuit current and ﻿RRR﻿ is the resistance measured between the output terminals.


The fact that these networks are equivalent, means that two things should also be true:

  • If we connect nothing to the terminals, the outputs should be the same: the open-circuit voltage ﻿VOCV_{OC}VOC​﻿
  • If we connect the output terminals together, each circuit should produce the same current: the short-circuit current ﻿ISCI_{SC}ISC​﻿


The resistance in the two equivalent circuits is given by ﻿R=VOCISCR=\frac{V_{OC}}{I_{SC}}R=ISC​VOC​​﻿.


Finding ﻿VOCV_{OC}VOC​﻿ and ﻿ISCI_{SC}ISC​﻿ for a real circuit can be done by taking measurements and extrapolating what would happen with infinite resistance (i.e. no current) or no resistance (i.e. no voltage)


3.9 Superposition

In any linear network of resistors, voltage sources and current sources, each voltage and current in the circuit is equal to the algebraic sum of the voltages or currents that would be present if each source were to be considered separately. When determining the effects of a single source, the remaining sources are replaced by their internal resistance. This is called the principle of superposition.

3.10 Nodal analysis

Nodal analysis is a systematic method of applying Kirchhoff’s current law to nodes in a circuit in order to produce an appropriate set of simultaneous equations.


The technique involves six distinct steps:

  1. One of the nodes in the circuit is chosen as a reference node. This selection is arbitrary, but it is normal to select the ground or earth node as the reference point, and all voltages will then be measured with respect to this node
  2. The voltages on the remaining nodes in the circuit are then labelled ﻿V1V_1V1​﻿, ﻿V2V_2V2​﻿, ﻿V3V_3V3​﻿ etc. Again, the numbering of these node voltages is arbitrary
  3. If the voltages on any of the nodes are known (due to the presence of fixed-voltage sources), then these values are added to the diagram
  4. Kirchhoff’s current law is then applied to each node for which the voltage is not known. This produces a set of simultaneous equations
  5. These simultaneous equations are solved to determine each unknown node voltage
  6. If necessary, the node voltages are then used to calculate appropriate currents in the circuit

3.11 Mesh analysis

Mesh analysis, like nodal analysis, is a systematic means of obtaining a series of simultaneous equations describing the behavior of a circuit. In this case, Kirchhoff’s volt age law is applied to each mesh in the circuit.


Again a series of steps are involved:

  1. Identify the meshes in the circuit and assign a clockwise-flowing current to each. Label these currents ﻿I1I_1I1​﻿, ﻿I2I_2I2​﻿, ﻿I3I_3I3​﻿ etc
  2. Apply Kirchhoff’s law to each mesh by summing the voltages clockwise around each mesh, equating the sum to zero. This produces a number of simultaneous equations (one for each mesh)
  3. Solve these simultaneous equations to determine the currents ﻿I1I_1I1​﻿, ﻿I2I_2I2​﻿, ﻿I3I_3I3​﻿ etc
  4. Use the values obtained for the various currents to compute voltages in the circuit as required
