Two level gate networks

They are important since they have a small latency and there exists a simple design procedure to find the minimum number of gates.


For sum of products:

  • Level 0: NOT (optional)
  • Level 1: AND
  • Level 2: OR


For product of sums:

  • Level 0: NOT (optional)
  • Level 1: OR
  • Level 2: AND


An example two level gate network:


Two level gate networks make a few assumptions (which are sometimes also limitations):

  • All inputs are available in complemented and uncomplemented form (so we can ignore the first layer of NOTs)
  • Gates have an unlimited fan-in (gates with larger fan-in usually have also larger delays. It is therefore impractical to use gates with large fan-ins)
  • The network only has 1 output (limitation!)
  • The cost optimization consists of finding the network with the minimum number of gates, if more than 1 exists, then we find the network with the minimum number of inputs


Some other limitations of gate networks:

  • "Squeezing" the functionality to only two levels might result in using larger amount of gates and connections. Moreover, the larger amount of connections need to be routed and can potentially lead to irregular connections.
  • In certain technologies, regularity of gates is preferred over minimizing the number of gates. In these cases, the minimization is of less importance


NAND-NAND/NOR-NOR networks

We can transform sum of products networks into NAND-NAND networks, which use less transistors:

  1. we replace the OR gate with a NOT-NOT-OR gate, which is equivalent to a NAND gate.
  2. we replace the AND gates with NAND gates in order to make sure that the signals are "inverted" and thus we end up with a correct result.


The same thing can be done with product of sum networks, to create NOR-NOR networks.



Multi-level NAND/NOR networks

Same procedure as previously:

  • Each AND is converted to NAND by inverting its output
  • Each OR is converted to NAND by inverting its inputs

Minimization procedure (sum of products)

We have to define a few terms:


Implicant: any product term for which ﻿f=1f=1f=1﻿ (in the image below, the red box is for example an implicant)


Prime implicant (PI): implicant not completely covered by another single implicant (the ﻿jjj﻿-value 15 is not a prime implicant, since it is also covered by the red box). They are the shortest product term candidates for the final expression


Essential prime implicant (EPI): prime implicant with ﻿111﻿’s not covered by any other prime implicant. If you leave it out, the function would be incomplete (the ﻿jjj﻿-value 5 and 7 for example).


We can conclude that the lowest-cost implementation is achieved when the cover of a given function consists of only prime implicants. All EPIs must always be included in the minimum expression.


Thus the procedure is:

  • Determine all prime implicants
  • Identify the essential prime implicants
  • Cover uncovered ﻿111﻿’s with remaining PIs (note that there may be multiple choices, where some choices may be more efficient than others)

Minimization procedure (product of sums)

We again have to define a few terms:


Implicant: any sum term for which ﻿f=0f=0f=0﻿

Prime implicant (PI): implicant not completely covered by another single implicant

Essential prime implicant (EPI): prime implicant with ﻿000﻿﻿111﻿’s not covered by any other prime implicant.


The procedure is exactly the same otherwise.


Don't care

We can place don’t cares in truth tables where the output value is unimportant, this allows for even more optimization. In a K-map, they can be circled if they help cover the ﻿000﻿'s or ﻿111﻿’s with fewer or larger circles, but they don’t have to be circled if they are not helpful.


Programmable Modules (PLA)

A standard (fixed) structure, that can be programmed for a particular function, either during fabrication or during installation. They’re flexible, but more expensive and slower than fixed-function modules.


They consist of the 2-level gate networks as we have seen before, and the connections can be programmed. The connections before the gates can be either enabled or disabled, based on the function that is implemented. They can also be made to use NOR-NOR or NAND-NAND structures.


There are also programmable array logic (PAL) devices, which have a programmable AND plane but fixed OR plane. They are simpler to manufacture, less expensive and offer better performance.
