Bandpass signals and systems

Modulation

Modulation is the method to adapt one or more parameters of a carrier signal according to an information signal, in order to make it suitable for transmission over the available channel.



After applying modulation and making it a bandpass signal, we get a double sided spectrum.


For baseband signals, ﻿∣f∣≤B\left|f\right|\le B∣f∣≤B﻿, so it is centered around ﻿000﻿.

For bandpass signals, ﻿fmin⁡≤∣f∣≤fmax⁡f_{\min}\le\left|f\right|\le f_{\max}fmin​≤∣f∣≤fmax​﻿, so it is between some min and max.


We apply modulation because:

  • Baseband signaling is not suitable for every medium.
  • Not enough space in baseband domain: distortion, interference
  • Signals can be stacked in the frequency domain: FDM = Frequency Division Multiplexing
  • Signals can be easily separated in the frequency domain

Transmission system

Transmitter

The transmitter transforms the information signal ﻿m(t)m\left(t\right)m(t)﻿ into a modulated carrier signal ﻿s(t)s\left(t\right)s(t)﻿ at frequency which is suitable for the available transmission channel.


We distinguish between two types of carrier signals:


Receiver

The aim of the receiver is to retrieve the information signal from the received bandpass signal (+ noise and interference) as accurately as possible at the destination.


RF tuned receiver

An RF tuned receiver is a type of radio receiver that is composed of one or more tuned radio frequency amplifier stages followed by a detector (demodulator) circuit to extract the audio signal and usually an audio frequency amplifier.


It works as follows:

We have an antenna, which converts the signal into a very small electrical signal. Then we have a bandpass filter to remove noise, an amplifier to increase the signal from a few ﻿μV\mu VμV﻿ to ﻿VVV﻿. After detection we get a detection of our original signal, which we again amplify.


Disadvantages:

  • difficult to tune to a different frequency (we need to change the bandpass filter)
  • feed-back problems due to high amplification (oscillation)
  • difficult to implement narrowband filters (sensitive to adjacent channel interference)


Superheterodyne receiver

A superheterodyne receiver uses frequency mixing to convert a received signal to a fixed intermediate frequency which can be more conveniently processed than the original carrier frequency.


As you can see, the oscillator multiplies the input signal with a different frequency.

We also need to keep in mind that the oscillator can resonate with the signal frequency, which is something we need to keep in mind. For this we perform up/down conversion, so we will amplify the signal at another frequency range to limit interference.


Something else to keep in mind is that there are image frequencies different than the sired signal that are also converted to the IF, which will distort the reception of the desired signal at frequency ﻿fcf_cfc​﻿. These signals need to be removed (attenuated) at RF level, before the mixer.

Homodyne receiver

In a zero_IF (homodyne) receiver, the signal is immediately converted to baseband: ﻿fIF=0Hzf_{IF}=0HzfIF​=0Hz﻿, ﻿fLO=fcf_{LO}=f_cfLO​=fc​﻿.


Advantages:

  • No image frequencies
  • LO-frequency is equal to the receive frequency
  • Broadly applicable and cheap
  • Via an AD-converter direct interface to a DSP
  • DSP software determines the receiver qualities


Disadvantages:

  • LO-signal leakage to the antenna
  • DC-offset after the mixer (e.g. due to LO leakage)
  • Low sensitivity: low-noise signal amplification needed

  4. Unwanted signals generated in the receiver
  5. non-linear distortion in the receiver front-end (amplifier, mixer), especially when strong input signals are present
  6. image frequencies (including those due to harmonic distortion in the LO)
  7. pass-through of signals outside the selected channel bandwidth due to a lack of selectivity
