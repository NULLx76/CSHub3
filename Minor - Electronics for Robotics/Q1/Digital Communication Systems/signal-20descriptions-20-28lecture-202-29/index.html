Bandwidth

Band-limited signals

A band-limited signal is a signal in which only some particular band of frequencies are present.  Band-limited signals are important in telecommunications because:

  1. Band-limited signals allow multiplexing in the frequency domain: efficient use of the available bandwidth.
  2. Time continuous band-limited signals can be completely represented by a set of discrete-time sample values: signal sampling


Definitions

There are multiple definitions of bandwidth (defined for a single-sided spectrum). On the left you will see the baseband signal (starts at ﻿f≈0f\approx0f≈0﻿) and on the right is this bandwidth moved to a different center):

  • Absolute bandwidth: contains the full energy of the spectrum

  • 3-dB bandwidth: the part of the signal which is within 3dB (~2) of the maximal value

  • Equivalent noise bandwidth: the width of a fictitious rectangular spectrum such that the power in that rectangular band is equal to the power associated with the actual spectrum. This is defined by ﻿Beq=∫0∞∣H(f)∣2∣H(0)∣2dfB_{eq}=\int_0^{\infty}\frac{\left|H\left(f\right)\right|^2}{\left|H\left(0\right)\right|^2}dfBeq​=∫0∞​∣H(0)∣2∣H(f)∣2​df﻿.

  • Null-null bandwidth: the band from the first zero before the maximum value, to the first zero after the maximum value

  • Bounded spectrum bandwidth: the band where the power has dropped with ﻿xxx﻿ dB in comparison with the maximum

  • Power bandwidth: the band which contains 99% of the total power of the signal, such that 0.5% of the power is on each side.
