Signals

Signals can be characterized in different ways:

  • Deterministic signals: mathematical signals such as the sinc pulse, which uses minimum bandwidth
  • Stochastic or random signals: for example noise, we can't define them but we can reason about them statistically
  • Continuous-time / discrete: always defined vs only defined at specific instants
  • Periodic: repeats after a certain ﻿ttt﻿


Signals can be considered as a function of time ﻿w(t)w\left(t\right)w(t)﻿ or as a function of frequency ﻿W(f)W\left(f\right)W(f)﻿. Both are used in telecommunications. The time-domain shows how a signal (e.g. amplitude) changes over time and the frequency-domain shows how much of the signal lies within each given frequency band over a range of frequencies.

Amplitude and phase

The Fourier transform has some symmetry properties:

  • the magnitude (amplitude) is even in terms of frequency, as it's positive for both positive and negative frequencies
  • the phase is odd in terms of frequency


Fourier transform theorems

Linearity

The Fourier transform is a linear operation, for which the superposition property holds. That is, for signals ﻿w1w_1w1​﻿ and ﻿w2w_2w2​﻿, and arbitrary constants: ﻿F(a1w1(t)+a2w2(t))=a1W1(f)+a2W2(f)F\left(a_1w_1\left(t\right)+a_2w_2\left(t\right)\right)=a_1W_1\left(f\right)+a_2W_2\left(f\right)F(a1​w1​(t)+a2​w2​(t))=a1​W1​(f)+a2​W2​(f)﻿

Change of scale

When a signal ﻿w(t)w\left(t\right)w(t)﻿ is given, the signal ﻿w(at)w\left(at\right)w(at)﻿ means the time is either compressed (﻿a&gt;1a&gt;1a>1﻿) or expanded (﻿a&lt;1a&lt;1a<1﻿). The scale change theorem states that the FT then becomes: ﻿F(w(at))=1∣a∣W(fa)F\left(w\left(at\right)\right)=\frac{1}{\left|a\right|}W\left(\frac{f}{a}\right)F(w(at))=∣a∣1​W(af​)﻿.


If you "shorten" your signal (﻿a&gt;1a&gt;1a>1﻿, time goes faster, signal is compressed), it will spread out in frequency, and vice versa. So a fast, short signal has a wide frequency domain.
