Telecommunications

Telecommunications means communicating over large distances. We will discuss these based on models (meta-system) instead of actual protocols.


We need to convert information at one source to suitable waveforms for transmission over an electro-magnetic channel which can be retrieved at the destination. Of course, we want to keep the possible distortion / error as low as possible.


Information sources

We make a distinction between analog and digital sources:


Analog: Analog sources generate a continuous range of levels, continuous in time.


Digital: Digital sources generate a finite set of discrete symbols (alphabet, ﻿MMM﻿ possible symbols), discrete in time (source rate ﻿RRR﻿, symbols/sec = baud rate). The advantage of using digital communication is the fact that we can use a single "channel" to transmit different types of information jointly (multiplexing) on cheap electronics. It is also relatively insensitive to noise, it's easy to detect and correct errors and encryption is easy. However, we do need more bandwidth than analog signals.

Channel capacity (Shannon channel)

How much information can you transmit across a transmission channel?


We can compute this using ﻿C=B⋅log⁡2(1+SN)C=B\cdot\log_2\left(1+\frac{S}{N}\right)C=B⋅log2​(1+NS​)﻿ (bits / s):

  • ﻿CCC﻿, capacity, the maximum error-free bit rate for a channel with Additive White Gaussian Noise
  • Additive: Independent of the signal
  • White: The noise is the same on all frequencies (Analogous to white light)
  • Gaussian: The noise has a gaussian distribution
  • ﻿BBB﻿, bandwidth of the channel (increasing the bandwidth has a big influence on the capacity)
  • ﻿S/NS/NS/N﻿, ratio of signal power to noise power (you have to increase your power a lot to increase your capacity)


Waveforms

Inference and distortion

There are a few sources of inference and distortion:

  1. Noise: an unpredictable process (cannot be removed)
  2. Interference: distortion due to non-relevant signals (intentional, system related, by accident)
  3. Distortion


So a waveform ﻿w(t)w\left(t\right)w(t)﻿ = received signal (unknown waveform) = desired signal (deterministic transmitted waveform) + undesired signal (noise + interference). This ﻿w(t)w\left(t\right)w(t)﻿ is usually a voltage or current.


Physically realizable waveforms satisfy several conditions:

  • Finite duration and finite energy
  • Finite bandwidth (no infinitely fast changes possible)
  • Continuous in time
  • Finite peak value
  • Real (not complex), although a complex representation is useful when discussing telecommunication signals and techniques

Modulation

Modulation: 

  • Adaptation of one or more parameters of a carrier signal (so you adapt a waveform to carry information to make it a carrier wave)
  • We need this to be able to transmit multiple signals on different frequency blocks (e.g. radio stations) or to choose the frequency which contains least noise/interference


Demodulation: Retrieval of the information from the received signal


For example, a modulated sine-carrier is ﻿s(t)=R(t)cos⁡(2πfct+ϕ(t))s\left(t\right)=R\left(t\right)\cos\left(2\pi f_ct+\phi\left(t\right)\right)s(t)=R(t)cos(2πfc​t+ϕ(t))﻿:

  • ﻿fcf_cfc​﻿ is the carrier frequency
  • ﻿R(t)R\left(t\right)R(t)﻿ is the instantaneous amplitude
  • ﻿ϕ(t)\phi\left(t\right)ϕ(t)﻿ is the instantaneous phase


If we change the amplitude over time: Amplitude modulation (AM)

If we change the phase over time: Phase modulation (PM) or frequency modulation (FM)
